\abstract{\addtocontents{toc}{} % Add a gap in the Contents, for aesthetics

Recent advancements in large language models (LLMs) have opened the possibility of assisting clinical practitioners in complex diagnostic scenarios. However, most existing evaluation protocols continue to rely on static question–answering datasets that fail to approximate the multi-stage, uncertainty-driven nature of real clinical practice. The absence of adaptive behavioural simulation, dynamic test-ordering capabilities, and context-sensitive patient responses limits the ecological validity of such benchmarks.

Motivated by these gaps, this project proposes an \textit{extended multi-agent clinical simulation framework} engineered from the ground up to evaluate LLMs under interactive, sequential, and bias-sensitive clinical conditions. Building on the conceptual idea of interactive diagnostic environments but implementing a significantly redesigned architecture, the framework comprises a modular orchestrator, a symptom-responsive dialogue engine, a diagnostic evidence aggregator, a bias-injection manifold, and a controller for sequential tool-based reasoning. The implementation was developed through several iterations of system-level refinement, with emphasis on robustness, extensibility, and compatibility with open-source LLMs.

The project further investigates how cognitive and implicit biases affect LLM-driven diagnostic reasoning, and proposes several architectural improvements—including hierarchical prompting layers, structured context management, and instruction-tuned behavioural stabilizers—to enhance reliability. Results placeholders are included for final accuracy, behavioural, and bias analyses.
